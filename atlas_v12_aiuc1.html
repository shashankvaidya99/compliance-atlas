The world's first compliance certification scoped specifically to AI agents. Where Volume IX governs the management system and Volume X governs the legal regime, AIUC-1 governs the agent's actual behavior — the layer the prior three AI volumes deliberately leave unaddressed. Published by the Artificial Intelligence Underwriting Company; technical contributors include MITRE, Stanford, MIT, OWASP, IBM Research, Gray Swan, Schellman, Anthropic, Google, Meta, and Microsoft. Seven companies certified as of May 2026.

Lanes — How to read

Vendor / agent operator AIUC + accredited auditor Insurance pillar

Two parties run the certification: the vendor implementing controls and the accredited auditor + AIUC technical testing team attesting against them. The insurance pillar is the differentiator — certification is the underwriting prerequisite for AI-agent liability cover.

Editorial note · How to read this volume

AIUC-1 is a first-mover commercial standard. AIUC writes the standard, runs the technical testing, accredits the auditors, and sells the insurance the certification enables. That vertical integration is the single most important caveat to surface up front — practitioners including Zack Korman (Pistachio CTO) and Lenny Zeltser (former Axonius CISO) have publicly raised concerns about the issuer-pays incentive structure. The contrary view (Phil Venables, Chris Hughes, Lena Smart) holds that the insurance liability creates a real counter-incentive against weak audits.

This volume covers the standard as it stands — what it requires, how it is audited, what evidence the auditor expects. The practitioner critiques live in Layer 03 alongside the evidence detail. Read both before recommending AIUC-1 to a client.

The AIUC-1 lifecycle — annual certificate, quarterly technical recurrence

How the lifecycle actually unfolds

The scoping questionnaire is where most of the assurance value is determined. AIUC-1 does not formally define "AI agent." Vendor and auditor decide which agent(s), which tools, which model versions, and which data flows fall inside the certificate. A certificate that scopes "the agent" without naming the specific deployment, model versions, and tool integrations is materially weaker than one that does. Any procurement reader receiving an AIUC-1 report should demand the scope section verbatim and verify it covers the deployment they will actually consume.

Technical testing is what differentiates AIUC-1 from SOC 2 and ISO 42001. The accredited auditor (Schellman) collects operational, legal, and evidentiary materials and writes the formal report. AIUC itself runs the technical adversarial testing — typically 2,000–5,000+ scenarios per certified agent, covering prompt injection, jailbreaks, hallucination triggers, cross-customer data leakage, tool misuse, and capability-specific risks (acoustic attacks for voice; image manipulation for image generation; multi-step workflow exploits for automation agents). For ElevenLabs's first certification: 5,000+ adversarial simulations. For UiPath: 2,000+ scenarios across IXP, Agents, and Autopilot. For Intercom: Air-Canada-style hallucination scenarios specifically tested.

Quarterly recurrence is mandatory and is the strongest mechanic in the framework. Annual certificates are familiar from SOC 2. What AIUC-1 adds: a re-test of the technical controls every three months. Models drift. Agents are retrained. Tool integrations change. The quarterly cadence is designed to keep the certificate honest between annual renewals. If a vendor fails the quarterly retest and cannot remediate, the certificate must be withdrawn.

5–10 weeks typical certification time for organizations with mature AI governance, per AIUC's own FAQ. Greenfield programs materially slower.

Schellman is currently the only accredited auditor (announced February 3, 2026 in a joint Schellman/AIUC press release; Schellman had previously become the first ANAB-accredited ISO 42001 certification body in September 2024). This is normal for a first-year regime — SOC 2 launched with limited auditor plurality in 2010. But until a second accredited auditor is named, every AIUC-1 certificate in the world is being shaped by one auditor-vendor relationship.

Auditor accreditation is by AIUC itself, not by an external accreditation body. Where ISO 42001 routes through ANAB / UKAS, SOC 2 through the AICPA, and EU AI Act notified bodies through EU-level accreditation, AIUC accredits its own auditors. This is the structural concern at the center of the practitioner critique surfaced in this volume's editorial note. HITRUST works similarly with its Assessor program — there is precedent — but it is a real departure from the ISO / regulatory model.

Phase deliverables

  1. Scoping — scoping questionnaire, agent inventory, tool integration map, model version log, data-flow diagram
  2. Gap analysis — 24-hour technical test report, gap-remediation plan, policy artifacts where templates were used
  3. Evidence — policy library, runbooks, logs, third-party-eval reports, risk-taxonomy documents, capability-specific testing evidence
  4. Technical testing — adversarial test plan, simulation results, vulnerability classifications (P0/P1/P2), remediation evidence
  5. Certificate — AIUC-1 certificate (12-month), full audit report under NDA, public badge, insurance eligibility
  6. Quarterly retest — re-run of technical scenarios with new attack vectors, drift detection, capability-creep check

Standard update cadence

AIUC-1 itself updates on a published quarterly schedule: January 15, April 15, July 15, October 15. The active version as of this Atlas is the Q2 2026 update (April 15, 2026) — focused on Model Context Protocol (MCP) security, agent identity/permissions, and third-party risk management. Two Accountability controls were retired in this update. Q3 2026 is in flight with 200+ peer-review comments carried forward.

AIUC-1 architecture — six domains, 51 active controls, 2 retired

Why the agent layer needs its own framework

AIUC-1 starts from a different operational assumption than its peers. ISO 42001 asks: does your organization have a management system for AI? NIST AI RMF asks: have you reasoned about AI risk? EU AI Act asks: does your AI system comply with EU law? AIUC-1 asks something narrower and operationally testable: what happens when a non-human actor with tool access takes autonomous action inside your business?

The agent as employee-equivalent control surface. Domain A's cross-customer exposure requirement, Domain B's "prevent unauthorized agent actions," Domain D's "restrict unsafe tool calls," and Domain E's "log AI system activity" together treat the agent the way a security program treats an employee: with named permissions, audit trails, accountable owners, and incident-response procedures specifically for what happens when the agent acts inappropriately. This framing is the conceptual reason AIUC-1 exists as a separate document from ISO 42001 or SOC 2 — neither addresses the non-human principal at this resolution.

Domain C is where most of the testing volume goes. Twelve controls; four of them require third-party adversarial testing. AIUC's published case-study testing volumes (5,000+ scenarios for ElevenLabs; 2,000+ for UiPath) reflect the depth Domain C demands. Practitioner-grade testing covers: prompt injection (direct and indirect), jailbreaks, hallucination triggers, customer-policy boundary violations, harmful-output generation, content-policy regressions, and capability-creep into out-of-scope behaviors.

Domain D is small but distinct. Four controls — but two are tool-call-specific. ISO 42001 has no comparable control. NIST AI RMF mentions tool use only in passing in the GenAI Profile. AIUC-1 makes tool-call governance a first-class testable requirement because that is where multi-step agent workflows go wrong.

Domain E carries the largest control count and the most retirement turnover. Seventeen controls with two retired in Q2 2026. The retired controls — "Document system change approvals" and "Share transparency reports" — were judged either redundant with adjacent management-system frameworks or insufficiently testable. The retirement itself is a meaningful signal: AIUC-1 is not a frozen standard. Auditors using AIUC-1 should monitor the changelog at every quarterly update.

Domain F is judgment-heavy. Two controls covering cyber misuse and catastrophic misuse. The Stanford / Center for AI Safety contingent of the consortium pushed for explicit floor coverage. Critics (Zeltser) note this is where documentation alone can satisfy requirements — what counts as sufficient mitigation for "catastrophic misuse" is fundamentally judgment-based, and auditors competing for repeat business may apply softer scrutiny over time.

Scoping note

Capability-based scoping means not every control applies to every agent. An internal text-only assistant scopes fewer controls than an externally facing voice agent with tool access and customer data exposure. The scoping questionnaire decides which controls are in-scope; the published certificate should always include this detail.

Evidence categories — operational, legal, technical, capability-specific

Common audit findings

AIUC's published case-study commentary and practitioner explainers (360 Advanced, CompliancePoint, SecureCoders) converge on a recurring pattern of audit findings:

  • Insufficient cross-customer isolation at the agent-context level (Domain A). SOC 2 addresses isolation at the infrastructure layer; AIUC-1 demands isolation at the agent-reasoning layer — that an agent serving Customer A cannot, under adversarial prompting, surface information learned while serving Customer B.
  • Under-defined customer-specific "high-risk output" taxonomies (Domain C). Vendors often define harmful outputs generically. AIUC-1 demands customer-defined high-risk categories — what a healthcare customer considers high-risk differs from what a financial services customer considers high-risk.
  • Missing failure-plan playbooks for hallucination-driven harms (Domain E). Security-incident playbooks are mature; hallucination-incident playbooks are not. The Air Canada precedent (court-ordered enforcement of a hallucinated refund policy) is the canonical scenario.
  • Weak logging of agent tool-call sequences (Domain E). Application logs typically capture API calls; agent audit trails need to capture the reasoning — which tool was called, why, with what authority, against what data, with what outcome.
  • Voice-agent–specific findings. Acoustic injection (audio modulated to inject prompts) and prosodic manipulation (emotion-driven manipulation) are novel attack classes most teams have no prior testing for. Articulated in the AIUC/Stanford/Gray Swan/ElevenLabs voice-extension paper.
A certificate is evidence, not warranty. AIUC's own FAQ: "No standard can eliminate all risks inherent in deploying AI systems, and certification represents a point-in-time assessment of controls, not a warranty of future performance or outcomes."

The practitioner critique — both sides

Korman (Pistachio CTO, May 2026): characterized AIUC-1 as "pure rent seeking" on LinkedIn, pointing to the structural conflict that AIUC writes the standard, runs technical testing, accredits the auditors, and sells insurance to certified vendors. Korman's affiliation with a competing AI-security firm is worth disclosing alongside the critique.

Zeltser (former Axonius CISO, April 22, 2026 blog at zeltser.com/aiuc-1-cert): extended Korman's critique with the issuer-pays credit-rating analogy: "AIUC itself accredits AIUC-1's auditors. Describing the framework as a 'standard,' therefore, rests on AIUC's own authority rather than an external accreditation body." Zeltser comes from a less commercially conflicted position.

AIUC's counter-argument: the insurance business creates a counter-incentive, because losses on certified agents hit AIUC's balance sheet directly. Phil Venables (former Google Cloud CISO), Lena Smart, Chris Hughes (Resilient Cyber), and the Schellman team all publicly endorse the framework.

Read both before recommending AIUC-1 to a client. The fair editorial reading: AIUC-1 is technically the most agent-specific compliance regime in existence, has unusually high-quality technical contributors, and is operationally useful for procurement teams. It is also a first-mover commercial standard from a venture-backed startup, with self-accreditation and a built-in conflict-of-interest profile that mature compliance regimes were specifically designed to avoid. Both are true.

The trap is treating the mappings as identities. They are overlaps, not equivalencies. AIUC-1's tool-call and adversarial-testing requirements have no clean equivalent in any other framework — which is exactly why it exists.

Where AIUC-1 is structurally additive

(i) Mandatory third-party adversarial testing tied to a market-readable certificate. SOC 2 has no testing requirement. ISO 42001 doesn't require technical adversarial work. NIST AI RMF is guidance. The EU AI Act requires conformity assessment for high-risk systems but doesn't prescribe adversarial test volume or cadence. AIUC-1 is the only framework where the certificate explicitly attests to thousands of completed adversarial scenarios.

(ii) Insurance-pairing. AIUC-1 certification is the underwriting prerequisite for AI-agent liability insurance distributed through AIUC. ElevenLabs's policy went live February 11, 2026. This is the only AI compliance framework directly paired with insurance, and it is where AIUC argues its quality counter-incentive comes from — losses on certified agents hit AIUC's balance sheet, so AIUC has reason to keep the certification rigorous. (Whether this incentive actually constrains audit quality is the open question Korman and Zeltser raise.)

(iii) Quarterly recurrence. Every other framework is annual or longer. AIUC-1's three-month technical retest mechanic addresses model drift, agent retraining, tool integration changes, and capability creep. This is the strongest pure-mechanism advantage in the framework.

(iv) The non-human principal as control surface. No prior framework explicitly treats the AI agent as an employee-equivalent with identity, permissions, audit logs, and incident-response procedures. AIUC-1 is the first to do so at requirement-level resolution.

Where AIUC-1 is weakest: the legal-regime layer (EU AI Act), the long-term operational management layer (ISO 42001), and the broad sectoral coverage (HIPAA, SOX, PCI). AIUC-1 should be adopted with these frameworks, not instead of them.

Emerging alternatives to watch

  • HITRUST AI Security Certification — announced 2024; regulated-industry-centric; ISO-style accreditation
  • CSA AICM — Cloud Security Alliance AI Controls Matrix; evolving toward STAR-style attestation
  • NIST IR 8596 — Cyber AI Profile (draft 2025); guidance form, not certification
  • EU AI Act conformity assessment — by 2027 mandatory for high-risk systems; will likely either reference or compete with private standards

None has a deployed cohort comparable to AIUC-1's seven certifications as of May 2026, but the space is moving fast. An auditor's library should track all four.

Auditor recommendations

Stage 1 (now): Add AIUC-1 to your AI-vendor due-diligence template alongside (not instead of) ISO 42001 / SOC 2 / GDPR. Demand the scope section verbatim on any AIUC-1 report you receive.

Stage 2 (≥2 accredited auditors named): Re-evaluate concentration risk. Single-auditor period is the weakest period in any new compliance regime's life cycle.

Stage 3 (ANAB/UKAS accreditation or regulator reference): Move from "AIUC-1 may help" to "AIUC-1 is the de facto baseline."

Stage 4 (post first high-profile incident): Watch the insurance carrier-of-record performance and public certificate handling. The framework either earns its credibility here or loses it.
